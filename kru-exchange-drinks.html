The Magic Starter Words
🌟 Three ways to start an order

Thai has three natural openers for ordering. All work — pick your comfort level.

Can I have... (polite, universal)
ขอ... ค่ะ/ครับ
khǎaw... khâ/khráp
✅ use this one
I want... (direct, casual)
เอา...
ao...
casual, street food
I'd like... (slightly softer)
อยากได้...
yàak dâai...
more considered
Core Ordering Phrases
01
Can I have one milk tea, please?
ขอชานมหนึ่งแก้วค่ะ
khǎaw chaa-nom nùeng gâew khâ
Sora: ขอ (khǎaw) = can I have / may I. หนึ่งแก้ว (nùeng gâew) = one glass/cup. แก้ว (gâew) is the classifier for drinks in cups — use it every time you count drinks.
02
Two cups, please.
ขอสองแก้วค่ะ
khǎaw sǎawng gâew khâ
Sora: Counting cups — หนึ่งแก้ว · สองแก้ว · สามแก้ว · สี่แก้ว · ห้าแก้ว (1–5). The drink name goes before the count: ชานมสองแก้ว = two milk teas.
03
What size do you have?
มีขนาดอะไรบ้างคะ?
mii khà-nàat à-rai bâang khâ?
Sora: มี...อะไรบ้าง (mii...à-rai bâang) = what [thing] do you have? Incredibly useful frame — swap in anything: มีรสชาติอะไรบ้าง = what flavours do you have?
04
I'll have the large size.
เอาแก้วใหญ่ค่ะ
ao gâew yài khâ
Sora: Sizes — แก้วเล็ก (gâew lék) = small · แก้วกลาง (gâew glaang) = medium · แก้วใหญ่ (gâew yài) = large. At Thai street stalls there's often only one size — just point and smile.
05
How much is it?
เท่าไหร่คะ?
thâo-rài khâ?
Sora: The most essential question in any Thai transaction. Short, works everywhere. If they tell you a number you don't catch: ขอใบเสร็จด้วยนะคะ (khǎaw bai-sèt dûay ná khâ) = can I have a receipt? — then you can read the total.
06
Do you have a menu?
มีเมนูไหมคะ?
mii me-nuu mǎi khâ?
Sora: Thai street stalls often have menus on boards behind the counter — point freely! ไหม (mǎi) at the end turns any sentence into a yes/no question.

Milk Tea Key Phrases
01
Can I have one Thai iced tea, please?
ขอชาเย็นหนึ่งแก้วค่ะ
khǎaw chaa yen nùeng gâew khâ
Sora: This is your go-to order at any Thai street stall. ชาเย็น (chaa yen) — literally "cold tea" — refers specifically to Thai orange tea with condensed milk over ice. It's ฿25–40 at most spots.
02
What kind of milk tea do you have?
มีชานมแบบไหนบ้างคะ?
mii chaa-nom bàaep nǎi bâang khâ?
Sora: แบบไหน (bàaep nǎi) = what kind/type. Swap ชานม for anything: มีกาแฟแบบไหนบ้าง = what kinds of coffee do you have?
03
Does it come with condensed milk?
ใส่นมข้นด้วยไหมคะ?
sài nom khôn dûay mǎi khâ?
Sora: ใส่ (sài) = to put in / to add. This verb is key for customising orders — ใส่น้ำแข็ง = add ice · ใส่นม = add milk. Its opposite: ไม่ใส่ (mâi sài) = without / don't add.
04
Without condensed milk, please — just fresh milk.
ไม่ใส่นมข้นนะคะ — ใส่นมสดแทนได้ไหม?
mâi sài nom khôn ná khâ — sài nom sòt thaen dâai mǎi?
Sora: แทน (thaen) = instead / as a substitute. ...ได้ไหม (...dâai mǎi) = is it possible? / can you? A very polite way to make a special request.

Temperature — ร้อน vs เย็น
🌟 Key insight — ร้อน and เย็น modify the drink name

In Thai, you swap the temperature word at the end of the drink name. ชาร้อน (hot tea) vs ชาเย็น (cold/iced tea). The same logic applies to coffee: กาแฟร้อน vs กาแฟเย็น. No extra words needed — it's elegant.

Using Temperature in Orders
01
Hot milk tea, please.
ขอชานมร้อนค่ะ
khǎaw chaa-nom ráawn khâ
Sora: Simple — just add ร้อน after the drink name. Works for everything: กาแฟร้อน · ชาร้อน · โกโก้ร้อน (hot cocoa)
02
Can I have less ice, please?
น้ำแข็งน้อยหน่อยนะคะ
náam khǎeng náwy nàwy ná khâ
Sora: น้อยหน่อย (náwy nàwy) — "a little less" — adding หน่อย softens the request and sounds more natural and polite than just saying น้อย alone. A very Thai way of asking.
03
No ice, please.
ไม่เอาน้ำแข็งนะคะ
mâi ao náam khǎeng ná khâ
Sora: ไม่เอา (mâi ao) = don't want / without. A shorter, more casual alternative to ไม่ใส่น้ำแข็ง — both work perfectly.
04
Ice on the side, please.
น้ำแข็งแยกนะคะ
náam khǎeng yâaek ná khâ
Sora: แยก (yâaek) = separate. Useful when you want to control how much ice goes in — especially good for longer journeys so the drink doesn't dilute.
05
Can you make it hot instead?
เปลี่ยนเป็นร้อนได้ไหมคะ?
bplìan bpen ráawn dâai mǎi khâ?
Sora: เปลี่ยนเป็น (bplìan bpen) = change to / switch to. Works for many corrections: เปลี่ยนเป็นแก้วใหญ่ได้ไหม = can you change it to a large cup?

Sugar Levels
🌟 Key word — หวาน (wǎan) = sweet

Everything revolves around หวาน (wǎan). Add น้อย (náwy) = less, มาก (mâak) = more, or ไม่ (mâi) = not. Modern Thai tea shops (like Cha Tra Mue, Gong Cha) now offer percentage sweetness levels — very handy.

Using Sweetness in Orders
01
Less sweet, please.
หวานน้อยหน่อยนะคะ
wǎan náwy nàwy ná khâ
Sora: Your most-used phrase in Thailand. Say it with every order until you calibrate what "normal" Thai sweet tastes like to you. Most vendors are very used to hearing this from foreigners.
02
Half sweet, no ice please.
หวานครึ่ง ไม่เอาน้ำแข็งนะคะ
wǎan khrûeng mâi ao náam khǎeng ná khâ
Sora: This is a full customisation in one sentence — sweet level + ice level. Thai ordering is flexible; you can stack as many requests as you like. The vendor will usually confirm back to you.
03
No sugar please — I'm watching my sugar intake.
ไม่ใส่น้ำตาลนะคะ — กำลังลดน้ำตาลอยู่
mâi sài náam-dtaan ná khâ — gam-lang lót náam-dtaan yùu
Sora: กำลัง...อยู่ (gam-lang...yùu) = currently doing something (present continuous). ลด (lót) = to reduce/cut. Useful beyond drinks: กำลังลดน้ำหนักอยู่ = I'm currently trying to lose weight.
04
Is it very sweet?
หวานมากไหมคะ?
wǎan mâak mǎi khâ?
Kru: Thais often find it surprising when foreigners ask this — sweetness is considered a positive quality in Thai food culture. A gentle question rather than a complaint is always better received.
Sora: The answer will likely be มีหวานนิดหน่อย (mii wǎan nít nàwy) = a little sweet — even when it's very sweet by outside standards!

Take-Away Essentials
🌟 Key phrase — กลับบ้าน (glàp bâan)

The literal meaning is "go home" but Thais use กลับบ้าน (glàp bâan) to mean take-away — as in, you're taking it home with you. Alternatives you'll hear: ห่อกลับ (hàw glàp) = wrap to go, or just เอากลับ (ao glàp) = take away. All mean the same thing.

Take-Away Phrases
01
Take-away, please.
เอากลับนะคะ
ao glàp ná khâ
Sora: The shortest, most natural way to say take-away. You can also say กลับบ้านนะคะ and it means exactly the same thing. Most stalls will automatically put it in a bag with a straw.
02
I'll drink it here.
ทานที่นี่ค่ะ
thaan thîi nîi khâ
Sora: ทาน (thaan) is the polite word used for both eating and drinking. กิน (gin) = eat/drink, but ทาน is more refined and appropriate in these situations.
03
Can I have a straw?
ขอหลอดด้วยนะคะ
khǎaw làwt dûay ná khâ
Sora: ด้วยนะ (dûay ná) = also / as well please. Adding this to any ขอ request makes it sound like a small, considerate ask rather than a demand. Very useful particle combo.
04
Can you put it in a separate bag?
ใส่ถุงแยกได้ไหมคะ?
sài thǔng yâaek dâai mǎi khâ?
Sora: Useful if ordering multiple drinks for different people. แยก (yâaek) = separate — you already met this word with น้ำแข็งแยก (ice on the side).
05
No plastic bag needed, thank you.
ไม่เอาถุงพลาสติกนะคะ ขอบคุณค่ะ
mâi ao thǔng phlaas-dtìk ná khâ — khàwp-khun khâ
Sora: Thais appreciate eco-consciousness — this is a natural thing to say and won't surprise anyone. Bring your own cup to a chain café and you'll often get a small discount too.

Handy Extras
01
What do you recommend?
แนะนำอะไรดีคะ?
náe-nam à-rai dii khâ?
Sora: แนะนำ (náe-nam) = to recommend. One of the most useful questions you can ask anywhere in Thailand — for food, drinks, tourist spots. Thais love making recommendations and will be delighted you asked.
02
I'm allergic to milk — do you have a dairy-free option?
แพ้นมค่ะ — มีตัวเลือกที่ไม่มีนมไหมคะ?
phâe nom khâ — mii tua-lûeak thîi mâi mii nom mǎi khâ?
Sora: แพ้ (phâe) = to be allergic to. Critical word to know. แพ้ถั่ว = peanut allergy · แพ้กุ้ง = prawn allergy. Pair with อาหาร (aa-hǎan) = food for broader questions.
03
This is delicious!
อร่อยมากเลยค่ะ!
à-ràwy mâak looei khâ!
Sora: อร่อย (à-ràwy) = delicious. Adding มากเลย (mâak looei) = so much / really, makes it feel genuinely enthusiastic. The stall owner will beam. Always worth saying.
